  <Slide title="Lesson 3 — Tactics I: Forks, Pins, Skewers, and Double Attacks">
    Tactics are short, forcing sequences that win material or create a winning
    attack. This week you learn to **spot** them — and **avoid** walking into
    them.

    By the end of class, you should be able to:

    - Define fork, double attack, pin, and skewer
    - Use knight and queen forks to win material
    - Recognize when a piece is pinned or skewered
    - Scan every move with a simple checklist before you play
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Key definitions">
    Tactics vocabulary you'll use all workshop:

    | Term | Meaning |
    |---|---|
    | **Tactic** | A short forcing sequence — often 1–3 moves — that wins material or creates a decisive threat |
    | **Double attack** | One move that creates two threats at once; the opponent can usually answer only one |
    | **Fork** | A double attack where one piece attacks two or more enemy targets |
    | **Pin** | A piece cannot move because a more valuable piece is behind it on the same line |
    | **Skewer** | Like a reverse pin — a valuable piece is forced to move, exposing a piece behind it |
    | **Loose (unguarded)** | A piece with no defender; a prime fork target |

    Last week you learned to **develop quickly**. This week you learn what to
    do when developed pieces start creating threats.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Why tactics matter">
    Most beginner games are decided by one or two tactical shots — not by
    long-term strategy.

    Tactics show up when:

    - Pieces are **developed** and pointing at the enemy
    - A piece is **loose** (undefended)
    - The king and queen sit on squares a knight can reach
    - Two pieces share the same file, rank, or diagonal

    If you finished Lesson 2's opening checklist, you're already set up to
    **find** tactics. If you skip development, you won't have pieces in place
    to punish mistakes.

  <Slide title="Double attacks — two threats, one move">
    A **double attack** means your single move creates two problems your
    opponent cannot fully solve.

    Examples:

    - Attack the king **and** a rook
    - Attack the queen **and** a bishop
    - Deliver check **and** threaten a capture

    If only one threat can be parried, you win material. The **fork** is the
    most common double attack in beginner chess.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Knight forks — the #1 beginner tactic">
    Knights are the best forking piece because they **jump** — their attack
    cannot be blocked.

    A knight on a central square often hits targets on opposite sides of the
    board at once.

    Best fork targets:

    - **King + queen** (wins the queen)
    - **King + rook** (wins the rook)
    - **Queen + loose minor piece**

    <PieceValue piece="knight" />

    Watch for enemy knights landing on **c7**, **d5**, **e5**, and **f7**
    near your king and queen.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Example — knight fork on c5">
    White to move. The knight on **d3** can jump to **c5**, attacking both
    the king on **b6** and the queen on **d8** at the same time.

    Black can save only one. White wins the queen.

    <ChessBoard fen="1k1q4/ppp5/8/8/8/3N4/PPP2PPP/4K3 w - - 0 1" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Queen double attacks">
    The queen combines rook + bishop movement, so it can fork along ranks,
    files, **and** diagonals.

    Queen forks often appear when:

    - The enemy king and a piece sit on the same line
    - A loose piece sits near the king
    - You give check and attack something else at the same time

    <PieceValue piece="queen" />

    Queen adventures from Lesson 2? This is why early queen moves backfire —
    a developed queen creates forks; a wandering queen **gets** forked.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Pawn forks — small piece, big surprise">
    Pawns attack diagonally, so a single pawn push can fork two pieces at
    once.

    Common pattern:

    - Enemy king on **c6**, knight on **e6**
    - **d4–d5+** attacks both

    Pawn forks are easy to miss because pawns feel slow. Always check what
    squares your opponent's next pawn push will hit.

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/2k5/4n3/3P4/8/8/4K3 w - - 0 1" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Pins — stuck in place">
    A **pin** happens when a piece cannot move without exposing a more
    valuable piece behind it on the same line (file, rank, or diagonal).

    Bishops and rooks create most pins. A pinned piece is often effectively
    frozen — or it becomes a free capture.

    Think: **"stuck in place."**

    <ChessBoard fen="r1bqk2r/pppp1ppp/2n5/4p3/1PB5/8/PPP2PPP/RNBQK2R b KQkq - 0 1" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Absolute vs relative pins">
    | Type | What stops the piece from moving? | Example |
    |---|---|---|
    | **Absolute pin** | Moving would expose the **king** to check — illegal | Bishop pins a knight to the king |
    | **Relative pin** | Moving would lose a **more valuable** piece | Bishop pins a knight to the queen |

    Absolute pins are stronger — the pinned piece truly cannot move. Relative
    pins can sometimes be broken, but it usually costs material.

    Good habit: if you spot a pin, ask **"Can I win the pinned piece?"**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Skewers — the reverse pin">
    A **skewer** looks like a pin, but the **more valuable** piece is in
    **front**.

    When that piece moves out of the way, you capture what's behind it.

    Think: **"forced to move."**

    Classic pattern: bishop checks the king on **b6**, and a rook on **c7**
    sits behind it on the same diagonal.

    <ChessBoard fen="8/2r5/1k6/B7/8/8/8/4K3 b - - 0 1" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Pin vs skewer — side by side">
    | | **Pin** | **Skewer** |
    |---|---|---|
    | Valuable piece | Behind (stays put) | In front (must move) |
    | Weaker piece | In front (frozen) | Behind (gets captured) |
    | Feeling | "I can't move" | "I have to move — and lose something" |

    Both use the same geometry: pieces lined up on a file, rank, or diagonal.
    Training your eye to see **lines** is the key to spotting both.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="How to find tactics — the CCT scan">
    Before every move, scan for:

    1. **Checks** — can I give check and win something?
    2. **Captures** — can I take something safely?
    3. **Threats** — can I attack an undefended piece?

    Say **"CCT"** quietly in your head. It takes five seconds and catches
    most beginner tactics — both yours and your opponent's.

    Also ask: **"What did my opponent's last move threaten?"** If you only
    look at your own ideas, you walk into forks.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Defending against forks">
    Prevention beats calculation. Habits that stop forks:

    - Keep king and queen on **different** knight paths when possible
    - Don't leave pieces **undefended** in the center
    - Castle so your king is not sitting on an open diagonal or file
    - Before moving, ask: **"What squares does the enemy knight reach?"**

    If a fork is already on the board, sometimes you must give up a pawn to
    save the queen. Losing a pawn beats losing the queen.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Defending against pins and skewers">
    - **Break the pin** — move the valuable piece behind, or block the line
      with another piece or pawn
    - **Challenge the pinning piece** — attack the bishop or rook that creates
      the pin
    - **Don't put king and queen on the same line** without a good reason
    - **Castle early** — a king stuck in the center is the easiest skewer target

    If your piece is pinned, treat it as if it is not on the board until the
    pin is broken.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Tactic checklist (use every game)">
    Before you move:

    - **CCT scan** — any checks, captures, or threats for me?
    - **Opponent CCT** — what are they threatening with their last move?
    - **Knight squares** — where could an enemy knight fork me?
    - **Lines** — any pins or skewers on open files and diagonals?
    - **Loose pieces** — is anything undefended?

    After the game, tag each big material swing: was it a fork, pin, or skewer?
  </Slide>
