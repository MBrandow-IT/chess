  <Slide title="Welcome to chess!" subtitle="Lesson 1 — Chess Basics">
    Chess is a game between two players. One plays the **white** army, the
    other plays the **black** army.

    Today we'll cover everything you need to play your first game:

    - The **board** and how to set it up
    - How each of the **six pieces** moves and captures

  <Slide title="64 squares, two colors">
    The board is **8 × 8 = 64 squares**, half light and half dark.

    Two setup rules to remember:

    - **"Light on right"** — the bottom-right corner is always a light square.
    - **"Queen on her color"** — the white queen starts on a light square,
      the black queen on a dark square.

    <ChessBoard fen="rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/8/8/PPPPPPPP/RNBQKBNR w KQkq - 0 1" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Files, ranks, and square names">
    Columns are called **files** and use letters **a–h** (a on White's left).

    Rows are called **ranks** and use numbers **1–8** (1 closest to White).

    Combine them to name any square: the white king starts on **e1**, the
    black king on **e8**, and White's favorite first move is to **e4**.

    <ChessBoard
      fen="rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/8/8/PPPPPPPP/RNBQKBNR w KQkq - 0 1"
      showCoordinates={true}
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The pawn (♟)">
    Pawns move **forward one square**. On a pawn's very first move it may move
    **two squares** if it likes.

    Pawns **capture differently from how they move** — one square
    **diagonally** forward. They can never go backward.

  <Slide title="The rook (♜)">
    The rook moves any number of squares in a **straight line** — up, down,
    left, or right. It can't jump over pieces.

    To capture, it just lands on the enemy piece.

    <PieceValue piece="rook" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/4R3/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="e4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The bishop (♝)">
    The bishop moves any number of squares **diagonally**. Each bishop stays
    on its starting color forever — light bishops always on light squares,
    dark on dark.

    <PieceValue piece="bishop" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/3B4/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="d4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The knight (♞)">
    The knight is the trickiest piece. It moves in an **L-shape**:
    **two squares in one direction, then one square perpendicular** — like
    a capital letter L.

    The knight is the **only piece that can jump over others**. It always
    lands on the opposite color from the square it started on.

    <PieceValue piece="knight" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/4N3/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="e4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The queen (♛)">
    The queen is the strongest piece. She combines the rook and bishop:
    any number of squares in a **straight line OR a diagonal**.

    Like the rook and bishop, she can't jump.

    <PieceValue piece="queen" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/3Q4/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="d4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The king (♚)">
    The king moves **one square in any direction** — straight or diagonal.

    The king is the most important piece. You **can never lose your king** —
    if it's attacked you have to deal with the threat (we'll see this in the
    next lesson when we talk about <Term name="check">check</Term> and
    <Term name="checkmate">checkmate</Term>).

  <Slide title="Recap — how every piece moves">
    | Piece | Move | Capture | Worth |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Pawn | Forward 1 (or 2 on first move) | Diagonally forward 1 | 1 |
    | Knight | "L" — 2 + 1, can jump | Same | 3 |
    | Bishop | Diagonals, any distance | Same | 3 |
    | Rook | Straight lines, any distance | Same | 5 |
    | Queen | Straight OR diagonal, any distance | Same | 9 |
    | King | One square in any direction | Same | priceless |

    Those **Worth** points are a quick way to know when a trade is good —
    capturing a knight (3) with a pawn (1) is great; losing your queen (9)
    for a bishop (3) is not. We'll dig into trades and material balance in
    the next lesson.

    You now know enough to play a full game! Next time we'll cover
    **check**, **checkmate**, and the three weird rules: castling, en
    passant, and pawn promotion.
