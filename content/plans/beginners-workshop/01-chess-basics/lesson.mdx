  <Slide title="Welcome to chess!" subtitle="Lesson 1 — Board, Pieces, and Rules">
    Chess is a game between two players. One plays the **white** army, the
    other plays the **black** army.

    Today we'll cover everything you need to play your first game:

    - The **board** and how to set it up
    - How each of the **six pieces** moves and captures
    - **Check**, **checkmate**, and three special rules

  <Slide title="64 squares, two colors">
    The board is **8 × 8 = 64 squares**, half light and half dark.

    Two setup rules to remember:

    - **"Light on right"** — the bottom-right corner is always a light square.
    - **"Queen on her color"** — the white queen starts on a light square,
      the black queen on a dark square.

    <ChessBoard fen="rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/8/8/PPPPPPPP/RNBQKBNR w KQkq - 0 1" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Files, ranks, and square names">
    Columns are called **files** and use letters **a–h** (a on White's left).

    Rows are called **ranks** and use numbers **1–8** (1 closest to White).

    Combine them to name any square: the white king starts on **e1**, the
    black king on **e8**, and White's favorite first move is to **e4**.

    <ChessBoard
      fen="rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/8/8/PPPPPPPP/RNBQKBNR w KQkq - 0 1"
      showCoordinates={true}
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The pawn (♟)">
    Pawns move **forward one square**. On a pawn's very first move it may move
    **two squares** if it likes.

    Pawns **capture differently from how they move** — one square
    **diagonally** forward. They can never go backward.

  <Slide title="The rook (♜)">
    The rook moves any number of squares in a **straight line** — up, down,
    left, or right. It can't jump over pieces.

    To capture, it just lands on the enemy piece.

    <PieceValue piece="rook" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/4R3/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="e4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The bishop (♝)">
    The bishop moves any number of squares **diagonally**. Each bishop stays
    on its starting color forever — light bishops always on light squares,
    dark on dark.

    <PieceValue piece="bishop" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/3B4/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="d4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The knight (♞)">
    The knight is the trickiest piece. It moves in an **L-shape**:
    **two squares in one direction, then one square perpendicular** — like
    a capital letter L.

    The knight is the **only piece that can jump over others**. It always
    lands on the opposite color from the square it started on.

    <PieceValue piece="knight" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/4N3/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="e4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The queen (♛)">
    The queen is the strongest piece. She combines the rook and bishop:
    any number of squares in a **straight line OR a diagonal**.

    Like the rook and bishop, she can't jump.

    <PieceValue piece="queen" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/3Q4/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="d4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The king (♚)">
    The king moves **one square in any direction** — straight or diagonal.

    The king is the most important piece. You **can never lose your king** —
    if it's attacked you have to deal with the threat right away. We'll cover
    exactly what that means in the next few slides.

    <PieceValue piece="king" />

    <ChessBoard fen="8/8/8/8/4K3/8/8/8 w - - 0 1" selectedSquare="e4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Recap — how every piece moves">
    | Piece | Move | Capture | Worth |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | Pawn | Forward 1 (or 2 on first move) | Diagonally forward 1 | 1 |
    | Knight | "L" — 2 + 1, can jump | Same | 3 |
    | Bishop | Diagonals, any distance | Same | 3 |
    | Rook | Straight lines, any distance | Same | 5 |
    | Queen | Straight OR diagonal, any distance | Same | 9 |
    | King | One square in any direction | Same | priceless |

    Those **Worth** points are a quick way to know when a trade is good —
    capturing a knight (3) with a pawn (1) is great; losing your queen (9)
    for a bishop (3) is not.

    You now know how every piece moves. Next up: **check**, **checkmate**,
    and three special rules every beginner needs — **castling**, **en passant**,
    and **pawn promotion**.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Check — your king is under attack" subtitle="Part 1 of 3">
    <Term name="check">Check</Term> means the **enemy is attacking your king
    on the very next move**.

    When you're in check, you **must** respond immediately. You cannot:

    - Ignore the attack
    - Make some other move and deal with it later
    - Move your king **onto** an attacked square

    In the position below, Black's queen on **g2** attacks White's king on
    **h1**. White is in check and must do something about it **this turn**.

    <ChessBoard
      fen="6k1/8/8/8/8/8/6q1/7K w - - 0 1"
      highlights={["g2", "h1"]}
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Check — move away or capture" subtitle="Part 2 of 3">
    Two of the three legal responses to check:

    **1. Move the king** to a safe square.

    **2. Capture** the attacking piece.

    Below, Black's rook on **e3** checks White's king on **e4**. White can
    flee to **d4**, **d5**, **f5**, or **f4** — or simply take the rook with
    **Kxe3**.

    <ChessBoard
      fen="4k3/8/8/4K3/8/4r3/8 w - - 0 1"
      highlights={["e3", "e4"]}
      selectedSquare="e4"
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Check — block the attack" subtitle="Part 3 of 3">
    **3. Block** the attack by sliding a piece between your king and the
    attacker — but **only** if the attacker is a **queen, rook, or bishop**
    attacking in a straight line.

    White to move. Black's queen on **h4** checks the king on **e1**. The
    king cannot capture the queen (a pawn on **g2** guards it). Can you block?

    <Puzzle
      fen="6k1/8/8/8/8/7q/6P1/4K3 w - - 0 1"
      solution={["g3"]}
      hint="Put a pawn on the diagonal between the queen and your king."
      title="Block the check"
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Checkmate — check with no escape" subtitle="Part 1 of 3">
    <Term name="checkmate">Checkmate</Term> is check where **none** of the
    three escapes work. The king is attacked and every possible response is
    illegal — the game is **over** on the spot.

    Remember the three escapes:

    1. Move the king
    2. Capture the attacker
    3. Block (if it's a sliding piece)

    If all three fail, that's checkmate.

    <ChessBoard fen="Q5k1/5ppp/8/8/8/8/8/6K1 b - - 0 1" highlights={["a8", "g8"]} />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Checkmate example — back-rank mate" subtitle="Part 2 of 3">

    Black's king on **g8** cannot step forward — pawns on **f7**, **g7**, and

  <Slide title="Castling — move the king and rook in one turn" subtitle="Special rules · 1 of 3">
    **Castling** is the only move where you move **two pieces at once**:

    - The **king** slides **two squares** toward a rook
    - That **rook jumps** to the square the king skipped over

    **Kingside castling** (short): king **e1 → g1**, rook **h1 → f1**.

    You may castle only if:

    - Neither the king nor that rook has moved yet
    - No pieces sit between them
    - The king is **not in check**, and does not pass through or land on an
      attacked square

    <ChessBoard
      fen="rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/8/5N2/PPPPPPPP/RNBQKB1R w KQkq - 2 3"
      arrows={[["e1", "g1"], ["h1", "f1"]]}
      highlights={["e1", "g1", "f1", "h1"]}
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="En passant — capture a pawn that just hopped two squares" subtitle="Special rules · 2 of 3">
    Normally pawns capture **diagonally**. **En passant** ("in passing") is
    the one exception:

    If an **enemy pawn** moves **two squares** from its starting rank and
    lands **right next to** your pawn, you may capture it **as if it had only
    moved one square** — but **only on the very next move**.

    Below, White's pawn sits on **e5**. Black just played **…d7–d5** (two
    squares). White may capture **en passant** with **exd6**, removing the
    black pawn on **d5** even though the pawn never stood on **d6**.

    <ChessBoard
      fen="4k3/8/3pP3/8/8/8/8/4K3 w - d6 0 1"
      lastMove={{ from: "d7", to: "d5" }}
      highlights={["d5", "e5", "d6"]}
    />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Pawn promotion — a pawn becomes a new piece" subtitle="Special rules · 3 of 3">
    When a pawn reaches the **far rank** (the 8th rank for White, 1st for
    Black), it **promotes**: you swap it for a **queen**, **rook**,
    **bishop**, or **knight** of the same color.

    Almost always you choose a **queen** — she's the strongest piece. But
    sometimes a knight fork is worth promoting to a knight instead!

    White to move. Push the pawn to **e8** and promote to a queen.

      hint="Advance the pawn to the last rank — it becomes a queen."
      title="Promote the pawn"
    />
  </Slide>
