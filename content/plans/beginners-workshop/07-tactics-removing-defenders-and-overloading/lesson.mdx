  <Slide title="Lesson 4 — Tactics II: Removing Defenders, Deflection, Overloading, and Finishing Attacks">
    Last week you learned to **spot** forks, pins, and skewers. This week you
    learn to **manufacture** tactics by attacking the pieces that hold the
    enemy position together.

    By the end of class, you should be able to:

    - Find the **defender** that guards a key piece or square
    - **Remove**, **deflect**, or **overload** that defender
    - Finish the job with a **back-rank** or mating-net attack
    - Chain two ideas together into a short winning **combination**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Key definitions">
    The vocabulary for this week:

    | Term | Meaning |
    |---|---|
    | **Defender** | A piece whose job is to guard another piece or a key square |
    | **Remove the defender** | Capture or trade off the guard, leaving the target undefended |
    | **Deflection** | Force a defender to move *away* from the square it is guarding |
    | **Overload** | A piece given two defensive jobs at once — it cannot keep both |
    | **Back-rank mate** | A rook or queen mates a king trapped behind its own pawns |
    | **Luft** | A "breathing hole" — a pawn move that gives the king an escape square |

    Tactics I asked **"is anything loose?"** Tactics II asks the deeper
    question: **"what is holding this position together — and can I knock it
    out?"**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="From Tactics I to Tactics II">
    A fork or pin only works if the target is **undefended** or **stuck**. Most
    of the time the target *is* defended — by exactly one piece.

    That single defender is the weak link. The whole lesson is one idea in
    three flavors:

    - **Remove** it — capture or trade the guard.
    - **Deflect** it — chase it off its post with a threat.
    - **Overload** it — give it more jobs than it can do.

    Knock out the guard, and the fork, pin, or capture you wanted suddenly
    works.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Every piece has a job">

    (highlighted). The knight is the guard — and White doubles rooks straight

    Find the guard first. Everything this week flows from that one question.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Removing the defender">
    The most direct method: **trade or capture the guard**, then take what it
    was protecting.

    Typical sequence:

    1. Identify the defender of your target.
    2. Capture or trade it off — often with **check** or a **threat** so the
       opponent cannot reorganize.
    3. Win the now-undefended piece, or mate on the newly weak square.

    The cleaner the forcing move (check first!), the less time your opponent
    has to bring up a new defender.
  </Slide>

    Notice how the **order** matters — capture the defender *first*.

  <Slide title="Overloading — one piece, two jobs">

    job, and it has to drop the other.

    cannot truly do both.

    To spot overloads, ask: **"is one piece the only defender of two different

  <Slide title="Finishing attacks — the back rank">
    Removing a defender wins material. **Finishing attacks** turn that edge
    into checkmate. The most common one in beginner games is the **back-rank
    mate**.

    It happens when a king is boxed in behind its own unmoved pawns:

    <SlideBoard slug="tactics2-backrank-board" />

    The king on **g8** has no escape — the pawns on f7, g7, and h7 wall it in.
    A rook or queen reaching the eighth rank is mate. The cure is **luft**:
    nudge a pawn early so your king can breathe.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Putting it together">
    Real combinations chain these ideas. A typical finish looks like:

    1. **Remove or deflect** the piece guarding the back rank.
    2. The opponent recaptures or steps aside — abandoning the key square.
    3. A waiting piece (a quiet bishop or second rook) delivers **mate**.

    The next puzzle is the full pattern. Look for the defender of **d8**, knock
    it out, and notice the bishop on **h5** quietly covering **f7** for the
    mate.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="How to find these tactics — the defender scan">
    Add one habit to last week's **CCT scan** (Checks, Captures, Threats):

    1. Pick the enemy piece or square you want to win.
    2. Ask **"what is defending it?"** — usually one piece.
    3. Ask **"can I remove, deflect, or overload that defender?"**
    4. Check whether the follow-up is a **capture** or a **mate**.

    Most beginner combinations are just CCT plus this defender question, two
    moves deep.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Defending — protect your defenders">
    The same lens keeps *you* safe. Before you move, ask the opponent's
    questions for them:

    - **Don't let one piece guard two things** — give every important piece a
      backup defender.
    - **Make luft early** — a quiet `h3` or `h6` stops most back-rank tricks.
    - **Watch for checks and captures** that hit your guards, not just your
      king.
    - If a defender is removed, **bring a new one** before grabbing material
      elsewhere.

    A position with no overloaded pieces and a king that can breathe is very
    hard to combine against.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Game-day checklist (use every move)">
    Keep this open while you play your games today. Before each move, run down
    the list:

    - **CCT scan** — any **C**hecks, **C**aptures, or **T**hreats for me?
    - **Find the guard** — what single piece defends the target I want?
    - **Knock it out** — can I **remove**, **deflect**, or **overload** it?
    - **Finish** — does a check, fork, or **back-rank** mate follow?
    - **My king** — do I have **luft**? Is any of my pieces overloaded?
    - **Their last move** — what did it stop defending?

    Five seconds per move. It catches almost every beginner combination — and
    stops you from walking into one.
  </Slide>
