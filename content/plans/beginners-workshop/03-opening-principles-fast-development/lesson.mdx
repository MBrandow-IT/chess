    Strong beginners follow principles instead of memorizing long move lists.

    Goals for today:

    - Fight for the center with pawns and pieces
    - Develop minor pieces quickly
    - Castle early and connect rooks
    - Punish opponents who ignore these ideas

  <Slide title="Key definitions">
    Before we start — a few terms you'll hear a lot:

    | Term | Meaning |
    |---|---|
    | **Development** | Moving a piece from its starting square to an active square where it controls the board |
    | **Tempo** | One turn of time. Wasting a tempo means giving your opponent a free move |
    | **Blunder** | A serious mistake that loses material or the game — marked with **??** in notation |
    | **Center** | The four key squares: **e4, d4, e5, d5**. Controlling them gives your pieces more room |
    | **Castling** | King safety move (reviewed last lesson). Get your king tucked away early! |
    | **Initiative** | Having the ability to create threats — the opponent has to react to YOU |
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Why openings matter">
    The opening sets up everything that follows. A good opening gives you:

    - **Space** — your pieces have squares to move to
    - **Time** — you develop faster than your opponent
    - **Safety** — your king is castled and protected

    A bad opening gives your opponent all three for free. Let's see how to
    avoid that.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Opening checklist for beginners">
    In your first 8–10 moves, aim to:

    - Control **e4, d4, e5, d5**
    - Develop knights and bishops once each
    - Castle before launching attacks
    - Avoid moving the same piece repeatedly without a reason
    - Avoid early queen adventures — she can get chased and you lose tempos

    <ChessBoard fen="r1bqkb1r/pppp1ppp/2n2n2/4p3/2B1P3/5N2/PPPP1PPP/RNBQK2R w KQkq - 4 4" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Common opening mistakes (blunders!)">
    - Moving too many pawns and falling behind in development
    - Bringing out the queen too soon (she gets chased — losing tempos)
    - Ignoring king safety
    - Chasing one piece while the opponent develops everything

    A simple plan beats random moves almost every time. When in doubt:
    **develop a new piece toward the center**.
  </Slide>
