    This week is all about building a reliable opening process instead of
    memorizing random moves.

    By the end of class, you should be able to:

    - Control the center with purpose
    - Develop pieces quickly and safely
    - Castle on time
    - Avoid common beginner traps
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Why openings matter">
    The opening sets the direction for the whole game.

    If your first moves are organized, you usually get:

    - Safer king
    - Better piece activity
    - Fewer tactical blunders
    - Easier plans in the middle game

    <ChessBoard fen="r1bqkbnr/pppp1ppp/2n5/4p3/3PP3/5N2/PPP2PPP/RNBQKB1R b KQkq - 2 3" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Opening priorities in order">
    A practical beginner order:

    1. **Fight for the center** (e4, d4, e5, d5)
    2. **Develop knights and bishops**
    3. **Castle**
    4. Connect rooks and improve your worst piece

    If a move does not help one of these goals, question it.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Center control wins space">
    Controlling central squares gives your pieces more active options.

    White has a strong center with pawns on **e4** and **d4**. Black should
    challenge that center quickly, not ignore it.

    <ChessBoard

  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Develop minor pieces before major pieces">
    Knights and bishops should usually come out before your queen and rooks.

    Development rules that save games:

    - Knights toward the center first
    - Bishops to active diagonals
    - Avoid moving one piece again and again without a tactical reason
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="King safety is a race">
    In many beginner games, the player who castles first has fewer blunders.

    Castling helps because:

    - The king leaves the center
    - A rook activates immediately
    - You can focus on plans instead of emergency defense

    <ChessBoard fen="r1bqk2r/pppp1ppp/2n2n2/4p3/2B1P3/2NP1N2/PPP2PPP/R1BQK2R w KQkq - 4 5" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Don't start a queen adventure too early">
    Early queen moves often lose time because your opponent can attack the queen
    while developing naturally.

    Good question to ask:

    **"If I move my queen now, can my opponent gain tempo by attacking it?"**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Trap awareness, not trap hunting">
    You do **not** need to memorize dozens of traps.

    You do need to remember:

    - Develop pieces before launching attacks
    - Be careful when f-pawns are weak
    - Check every forcing move: checks, captures, threats

    Trap awareness is defense first, not "hope chess."
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Scholar's Mate warning signs">
    Typical pattern: White targets **f7** early with queen + bishop.

    Warning signs for Black:

    - White queen appears on **h5** or **f3** early

    - Your king is still in the center

    Use this checklist in games this week:

    - Did I fight for the center?
    - Did I develop both knights and at least one bishop?
    - Did I castle before move 10?
    - Did I avoid unnecessary queen moves?
    - Did I check my opponent's threats every turn?
