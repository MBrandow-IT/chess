  <Slide title="Lesson 5 — Endgame I: Finishing Wins and King Activity">
    An **endgame** starts when most pieces are traded off and the kings come
    out to fight. This week you learn skills that win games you already
    deserve to win.

    By the end of class, you should be able to:

    - Deliver **checkmate** with king and queen vs king
    - Use **ladder mate** with two rooks to push the king to the edge
    - Avoid the stalemate trap when you are winning
    - Activate your king as a fighting piece
    - Spot **passed pawns** and simple pawn races
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Key definitions">
    Endgame vocabulary for this week:

    | Term | Meaning |
    |---|---|
    | **Endgame** | The final phase — fewer pieces, kings in the center, pawns matter more |
    | **Checkmate** | The enemy king is in check and has no legal escape — the game is over |
    | **Stalemate** | It is your turn, you are **not** in check, but you have **no legal moves** — the game is a **draw** |
    | **Passed pawn** | A pawn with no enemy pawns blocking it on the same file or on the files next to it |
    | **Promotion** | When a pawn reaches the far rank and becomes a queen (or another piece) |
    | **Opposition** | Kings face each other with one square between them; the side **not** to move often has an advantage |
    | **Ladder mate** | Two rooks (or a rook and queen) take turns giving check, pushing the enemy king toward the edge like rungs on a ladder |

    Last month you learned tactics to **win material**. Endgames teach you how
    to **convert** that advantage into a full point.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="What changes in the endgame?">
    In the opening and middle game, the king hides behind pawns. In the
    endgame, the king becomes a **strong piece** — like an extra minor piece.

    Typical endgame signs:

    - Most queens and heavy pieces are gone
    - Pawns become the main attackers
    - The king walks toward the center and supports pawns
    - One small mistake can turn a win into a draw — or a loss

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-king-pawn-intro-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Why endgames matter for beginners">
    Many beginner games follow the same story:

    1. You win the opponent's queen with a tactic
    2. You grab a few more pawns
    3. You **cannot find checkmate** and the game drags on
    4. You accidentally stalemate or blunder the queen back

    Learning king-and-queen checkmate fixes step 3. Learning stalemate
    awareness fixes step 4. King activity fixes pawn endgames where every
    square counts.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="When you are up a queen — finish the game">
    If you have **king + queen** vs **king**, you should win **every time**
    with correct technique. There is no excuse for a draw here.

    Your job is not to chase the king randomly. Your job is a **plan**:

    1. Use the **queen** to shrink the enemy king's living space
    2. Walk your **king** closer to help at the finish
    3. Push the enemy king to the **edge** of the board
    4. Deliver **checkmate** — without stalemating

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-queen-finish-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="King + Queen vs King — the big picture">
    Think of the queen as a **fence**. Each queen move builds a smaller
    box around the enemy king.

    Your own king is the **helper**. The queen alone can push the enemy king
    to the edge, but your king must get close for the final checkmate.

    <PieceValue piece="queen" />

    Rule of thumb: **queen builds the box, king closes the door.**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Stalemate — the #1 way wins disappear">
    <Term name="stalemate">Stalemate</Term> is when the side to move is **not**
    in check but has **zero legal moves**. The game is a **draw** — even if
    you were winning by a queen!

    When you are ahead, never take every enemy piece and every escape square
    unless the king is actually in **checkmate**.

    Ask before every winning move: **"Does my opponent still have a legal
    move?"** If not, it is stalemate — not victory.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Stalemate trap — what NOT to do">
    Black to move below. White's queen sits on **g7**, White's king on **g6**,
    and Black's king is trapped on **h8** — but Black is **not** in check.

    Black has no legal moves. That is **stalemate**. White threw away a win.

    When you push a king to the edge, leave it one escape square until you
    are ready to give **checkmate**.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-stalemate-trap-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Correct checkmate on the edge">
    Same corner idea — but now White delivers **checkmate**, not stalemate.

    The queen gives check from **f7**. The king on **g6** guards escape
    squares. Black's king on **h8** has nowhere to go.

    Notice: the queen is **not** right next to the enemy king on **g7** (that
    was the stalemate square). **f7** checks and covers **g8**.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-mate-on-edge-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 1 — shrink the box with your queen">
    Place the queen so the enemy king has fewer and fewer squares.

    Good queen habits:

    - Stay roughly a **knight's move** away from the enemy king when
      restricting — you control many escape squares without touching the king
    - Use the queen like a **wall** on a rank, file, or diagonal
    - Do **not** let your queen get too close and accidentally stalemate

    After each queen move that shrinks the box, look for a safe **king** move
    that steps toward the action.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-queen-shrink-box-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 1 in action — cut the king off">
    White to move. The black king sits on **e4**. A queen move to **e5** (or
    **d5**) builds a wall — the king cannot cross to the other half of the
    board.

    This is the first step in almost every king-and-queen win: **limit the
    king's territory**.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-queen-cutoff-demo-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 2 — walk your king forward">
    The queen does the chasing. Your king does the **marathon**.

    Move the king **one step** toward the enemy king whenever it is safe. You
    do not need to rush — just keep getting closer.

    In the position below, White's king on **c3** supports the finish while
    the queen on **c5** keeps Black boxed in on **c8**.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-queen-king-walk-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 3 — push the king to the edge">
    Repeat the pattern: **queen restricts → king walks → queen restricts**.

    The enemy king gets pushed toward the **side** of the board — the **a**-,
    **h**-, **1**-, or **8**-rank or file. Corners are the easiest place to
    finish.

    Below, Black's king is trapped on the **a**-file edge. White's queen and
    king work together to close the net.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-queen-push-edge-board" />
  </Slide>

    3. Push the enemy king to the edge
    4. Checkmate — check for stalemate first!

  <Slide title="Next up — ladder mate with two rooks">
    Queen checkmate uses one piece as a **fence**. Two rooks work the same way
    — but they team up like **rungs on a ladder**.

    If you have **king + two rooks** vs **king**, you should win every time,
    just like king and queen. The idea is familiar:

    1. **Cut off** the enemy king
    2. **Push** it to the edge with coordinated checks
    3. Deliver **checkmate**

    Many students trade down to two rooks after winning the queen. This
    pattern finishes those games cleanly.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="King + two rooks vs king">
    Two rooks are worth about as much as a queen — but they checkmate
    differently. Instead of one queen building a box, the rooks **take turns**
    giving check and shrinking the king's space.

    Your own king can help at the end (just like queen mate), but two rooks
    can often force mate on their own once the enemy king is trapped on the
    back rank or a-file.

    <PieceValue piece="rook" />

    Rule of thumb: **one rook cuts off, both rooks climb the ladder.**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="What is ladder mate?">
    **Ladder mate** means two rooks (or rook + queen) stand on **parallel**
    ranks or files and **alternate checks**, pushing the enemy king one step
    closer to the edge each time.

    Think of each check as stepping up one **rung**:

    - One rook gives check from far away
    - The king sidesteps
    - The other rook gives check from the same direction
    - Repeat until the king is on the back rank or **a**-/ **h**-file

    Same goal as queen mate: **limit first, finish second.**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 1 — cut off the king (same idea as the queen)">
    Start by using one rook as a **wall** on a rank or file — exactly like
    **Qe5** in queen mate.

    Below, White plays **Ra5**. The rook controls the whole **5th rank**.
    Black's king on **e4** cannot cross to the upper half of the board.

    Cut the king off **before** you start the ladder.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-rook-cutoff-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 2 — build the ladder">
    Once the king is cut off, place your rooks on **nearby ranks or files**
    so they can take turns checking.

    They work like fence posts on the same side of the board — one higher,
    one lower — closing the net with each check.

    Below, both rooks sit on the **5th rank** while Black's king is already
    trapped on the **8th rank**.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-rook-ladder-build-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Step 3 — alternate checks up the ladder">
    Each checking rook move pushes the king **one step** toward the edge.

    In the position below, Black's king is on **h6**. White can play **Ra6+**
    — the rook on **a1** checks from the side and forces the king toward the
    **8th rank**.

    After the king moves, the other rook checks from a new rung. Climb until
    the king is cornered.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-rook-ladder-check-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Ladder mate on the back rank">
    When the king reaches the back rank, the rooks finish with a final
    checking move.

    Below, Black's king is on **h8**. White's rooks on **f6** and **g6** are
    ready. **Rh6#** ends the game — the king cannot escape to **g8** or **g7**.

    Notice both rooks work together on the same side of the king, like the
    last two rungs of a ladder.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-rook-ladder-mate-demo-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Queen mate vs ladder mate — quick compare">
    | | **King + queen** | **Two rooks (ladder)** |
    |---|---|---|
    | Main tool | One queen builds a **box** | Two rooks **alternate checks** |
    | King needed? | Yes — for the final mate | Helpful, but rooks often finish alone |
    | Stalemate risk | High — watch closely | Lower, but still possible |
    | When you see it | After winning the queen | After trading queen for two rooks |

    Both patterns share the same lesson: **restrict the king, then finish.**
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="King activity in pawn endgames">
    When most pieces are traded, **activate your king early**.

    Priority list in king-and-pawn endgames:

    1. **Centralize** the king — toward **e4**, **d4**, **e5**, or **d5**
    2. Support your **passed pawns**
    3. Block or capture enemy passed pawns
    4. Use **opposition** when kings face each other

    The king below on **e3** supports the d-pawn and fights Black's king on
    **e5**.

  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Passed pawns — pawns that can run">
    A **passed pawn** has no enemy pawn blocking it on its own file or the
    files on either side. It can march toward promotion if the king supports
    it.

    Passed pawns are endgame **threats**. Even if they are far from promotion,
    they force the enemy king to react.

    Below, White's **d4** pawn is passed — nothing stops it from running to
    **d8** except Black's king.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-passed-pawn-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Opposition — kings face to face">
    When two kings stand on the same rank, file, or diagonal with **one
    square** between them, the side **not** to move often controls the key
    squares in front.

    That is **opposition**. It shows up constantly in king-and-pawn endings.

    Below, if it is Black's turn, White's king on **e4** controls **d5** and
    **f5** — Black may have to give ground.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-opposition-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="The square rule — can the king catch the pawn?">
    Draw an imaginary **square** from a pawn to its promotion square. If the
    **enemy king can step inside that square** on its turn, it can usually
    catch the pawn. If the king stays **outside**, the pawn often promotes.

    Fast visual check before a pawn race — no deep calculation needed.

    Count diagonally from the pawn to the promotion square. That box tells
    you whether the chase is winning or losing.
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Square rule — winning pawn race">
    White's pawn on **d3** is racing. Black's king on **e2** looks close — but
    count the square from **d3** to **d8**.

    If the king cannot enter that square in time, **push the pawn** and
    promote.

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-square-rule-win-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Square rule — king catches the pawn">
    Same idea, different result. Black's king on **b4** steps into the pawn's
    promotion square in time. The pawn on **d4** will **not** promote if
    White pushes it now.

    Before you push a passed pawn, ask: **"Can the enemy king enter the
    square?"**

    <SlideBoard slug="endgame1-square-rule-lose-board" />
  </Slide>

  <Slide title="Endgame checklist">
    Use this in games and practice this week:

    **When you are up material:**

    - Do I have a **plan** to checkmate, not just random checks?
    - With a queen: am I **shrinking the box**?
    - With two rooks: am I **climbing the ladder** with alternating checks?
    - Am I avoiding **stalemate**?
    - Is my **king** helping, not sitting on the back rank?

    **In pawn endgames:**

    - Is my king **centralized**?
    - Do I have a **passed pawn**?
    - Can the enemy king enter the pawn's **promotion square**?
    - Can I take **opposition**?
  </Slide>
